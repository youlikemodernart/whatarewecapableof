Reading

What an internal AI operating system means

An internal AI operating system is a controlled way for a company to use AI inside its existing work. It can retrieve approved information, summarize files, compare options, draft follow-up materials, extract fields, rank opportunities, and route questions to the right person.

The first version should fit the way Eidi already works. Yardi can remain the accounting backbone. Existing spreadsheets, manuals, notes, meetings, emails, and deal materials can become inputs where appropriate. The system should help around those sources before anyone considers replacing a core tool.

A good first system is narrow.

01

One workflow.

02

One responsible owner.

03

One clear input source.

04

One output the team can judge.

05

One approval rule before anything external happens.

That shape keeps the first project practical. It also creates proof. If a small workflow saves time, reduces repeated work, or makes a decision clearer, the next workflow becomes easier to choose.

Why source of truth comes first

AI work gets stronger when the business knows which source owns each fact. A source of truth is the maintained place where a specific kind of information is updated, trusted, and reviewed.

Information typePossible source of truthCommon downstream uses
Tenant and accounting recordsYardi or approved exportReports, exceptions, collections review, finance questions.
Property availabilityInternal property spreadsheet or approved databaseWebsite, broker follow-up, leasing sheets, internal status.
Deal criteriaInvestment memo, underwriting template, or criteria sheetDeal ranking, broker email review, acquisition triage.
Lease termsLease files plus extracted summary tableRenewal tracking, obligation review, clause questions.
Internal proceduresManuals or approved knowledge baseStaff questions, onboarding, recurring task support.

Without a source of truth, automation can still produce output, but the team spends time checking which version is right. With a source of truth, an AI system can work from the right facts and explain where each answer came from.

Discovery questions

A useful discovery exercise is to pick one workflow and answer five questions.

01

Identify where this information lives today inside the company.

02

03

04

05

Context management

AI systems perform better when they receive the right context for the task. Context includes instructions, approved files, examples, tools, constraints, and success criteria.

A broad prompt creates broad output. A narrow prompt with the right source packet creates work the team can review.

NeedRole
Specific task"Rank these five deals against our criteria" is stronger than "help us find deals."
Approved sourcesThe files, exports, pages, or notes the system can use.
Uncertainty ruleWhen the system should ask a question or mark a field unknown.
Human review stepWho checks the output before it affects a decision or leaves the company.
Evidence recordLinks, file names, timestamps, or row numbers that show where the answer came from.

This is the part that separates controlled internal tooling from casual AI use. The model matters, but the surrounding system matters more: what it can see, what it can do, what it must avoid, and who approves the result.

How to choose the first pilot

The first pilot should be small enough to prove value in one to two weeks. It should use safe data, avoid production-system changes, and create an output the team already understands.

Score each candidate from 1 to 5 on the criteria below.

CriterionQuestion
FrequencyHow often does this work happen?
PainHow much time, frustration, or risk does it create?
Data readinessCan we get safe sample inputs quickly?
RiskHow sensitive is the data or action?
Measurable outputCan the team judge success within two to four weeks?
Owner clarityIs there one person who can approve, test, and give feedback?

A strong first pilot has high frequency, high pain, available sample data, low operational risk, and a clear owner.

Data safety before access

The first project should classify data before requesting files or credentials. Commercial real estate workflows can touch leases, rent rolls, accounting records, tenant information, payment data, broker communications, and acquisition pipeline details. Those categories need explicit handling rules.

ClassExamplesStarting rule
PublicWebsite pages, public listings, public articles.Safe for research and examples.
Internal non-sensitiveProcess manuals, blank templates, non-confidential checklists.Safe after Eidi approval.
ConfidentialLeases, deal packets, tenant contacts, broker emails, underwriting files.Use only with explicit approval and a defined purpose.
Financial or accountingYardi exports, rent rolls, balances, payment status.Finance approval required.
Payment-adjacentPayment forms, card data, processor settings.Avoid in first pilot unless explicitly scoped.
Off limitsAnything Eidi marks private, privileged, contractual, or regulated.Exclude from prototype.

Handling modes

For each data class, the team chooses one handling mode.

01

Public-only research.

02

Redacted sample files.

03

Synthetic examples that imitate the structure without real client data.

04

Local-only processing.

05

Approved external model use.

06

No AI processing.

The right mode can vary by workflow. A public property cleanup may be safe with public pages and client confirmation. A lease or Yardi pilot needs a tighter approval path.

Email needs its own approval rule. Reading email, drafting replies, and sending messages are separate actions. A prototype can discuss the workflow before any message is shared, drafted, or sent. External model uploads also need explicit approval by data class.
