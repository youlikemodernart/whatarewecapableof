What you'd have

One private database holds every artwork and every collector. Every artwork: artist, title, medium, dimensions, edition, provenance, pricing, consignment terms, current location (main gallery, Aftermarket, or out at a fair), status, exhibition history. Every collector: who they are, what they've bought, what they've asked about, which fairs you've met at (Intersect, Scottsdale Ferrari Week, others), what they've passed on, when you last spoke. Any record is one search away.

An AI agent sits on top of it. It has context on the roster, the exhibition calendar, and the collector network. Before a fair, it pulls the relevant collector segment and drafts personalized outreach. You edit and send. When a new piece arrives, it prepares the record. You approve. When you ask "who's interested in Ramsey Dau and hasn't heard from me in six months," it answers.

Private catalog views live at URLs you share with specific collectors. You build a page for one person in thirty seconds; they see exactly what you chose to show them.

Artsy can stay synced once Georgie confirms the Partner API path. The goal is that artwork records update from the database instead of through separate re-entry.

Why not the alternatives

Georgie recommended ArtCloud because it matches what a lot of galleries at your scale use. Two things about both platforms make them weak fits for this specific goal.

01

Neither has a real API. Data goes in through Excel or CSV files and comes out the same way. The AI can prepare the file, but a person still imports it. After the initial load, every update runs the same loop: export, edit, import. That's the loop this system is meant to avoid.

02

Neither platform stores collector intelligence at the depth you described: relationship notes, passed-on works, fair context, outreach timing. Connecting a Paris conversation to a piece coming back on consignment takes a data model built to your practice.

Interfaces

Gallery web app

Private app at a BELHAUS subdomain. Daily-use interface for you and Chloe. Search any artwork or collector. Log a new piece or contact in two fields the system fills out. Pull up a collector profile before a call. See what's where. Monochrome design that matches the site.

Airtable

Direct access to the database for power editing, bulk changes, custom views. Useful for Chloe when she's cleaning up data in volume. Most daily work happens in the app; Airtable is the back room.

Private catalog URLs

A page you can build in thirty seconds and share with a specific collector: "Five works I thought you'd want to see." The link shows exactly what you selected, with your commentary, at your domain. You can see when they opened it.

The AI agent

The agent reads from Airtable, drafts into the web app, and sends nothing without you. It has three jobs.

Prepare

New artwork coming in: the agent drafts the record from whatever source you hand it (an invoice, a photo, an email from the artist). You approve. New collector met at a fair: the agent creates the profile from a business card photo or a voice memo. You approve.

Surface

Before a fair, the agent pulls the relevant collector list and drafts outreach for each. Mid-conversation with a collector, the agent surfaces the last five pieces they asked about, who they asked about them with, what they bought last. Quarterly, the agent reviews the collector base and flags stale relationships with next-step suggestions.

Draft

Press releases, social captions, SEO descriptions, artist spotlights, private catalog blurbs. The agent produces a draft in your voice. You edit. Whatever you don't want drafted, it doesn't draft.

The agent proposes; you decide. Sending emails, changing pricing, publishing content: everything outward-facing requires your approval.

What's portable

The data model uses standard field names. If you ever want to move to Artlogic or ArtCloud, a one-time export matches their schema. You own the records, the images, and the history. Nothing's locked in.

Three phases. The first produces a populated database. The second puts the full system in daily use. The third extends capabilities based on what proves useful.

Phase 1

Data audit and initial population

Before any system goes up, we find out what data exists and what's missing. Decisions made early: what counts as a collector versus a contact, how consignment terms get stored, which artwork fields matter most. Output: a starting dataset, complete where complete, flagged where incomplete.

  • Current belhaus.co listings (artworks, artists, exhibitions)
  • Any Artlogic exports from the brief populate attempt
  • Email threads with collectors and buyers
  • Phone contacts and SMS threads
  • Fair sign-up sheets
  • Spreadsheets and docs you've kept
  • Artist CVs and biographies

Duration: 1 week. You and Chloe field questions. Noah does the collection and preparation.

Phase 2

Build the database, the interface, and the agent

The database schema goes into Airtable, populated from the Phase 1 dataset. The web app ships as a functional v1: search, view, edit, log new records, private catalog generation. The agent comes online with its core capabilities: prepare new records, surface collector intelligence, draft outreach.

By the end of this phase, the system is in use. You and Chloe log in, do real work, and the AI drafts real emails.

Duration: 4 to 5 weeks.

Phase 3

Extend based on use

Capabilities that weren't in the initial v1 come online as they prove useful. Each gets scoped separately once we know it's needed.

  • Private catalog analytics (who opened the link, what they lingered on)
  • Outreach sequences, where a draft goes into a sequence instead of a one-off email
  • Press release and social drafting, tuned to your voice
  • Cross-business reporting with Aftermarket's Square data
  • Email newsletter migration off Squarespace Campaigns, if and when it matters

Duration: ongoing, at your pace.
