What can we fix on Cargo?

Some wins do not need a new site. We do these first either way.

01

Claim and optimize the Google Business Profile

The single biggest local lever does not touch the website. We make sure the studio's Google Business Profile exists, is verified, and is built out, so the studio shows up in the map and local results for Scottsdale and Phoenix searches. This works on any platform.

02

Put a real address on the site

The contact page shows only forms right now: no address, no phone, no city or state on the page. Adding consistent name, address, and phone is a core local-search signal, and it is straightforward to add on Cargo.

03

Rewrite the titles and the one description

Every page shares a single, identical description. We rewrite the page titles and that description toward the words people search, like Scottsdale interior design and custom home remodel, instead of page names. Cargo's control here is limited, but the wording is worth improving.

04

Clean up what Google sees

The sitemap lists 173 URLs, more than 40 of which are navigation, footer, archive, and test pages with no reason to be in search. We strip those out and ask Google to drop them, so the real pages stand on their own.

That is a huge tune-up and worth doing, but it has a ceiling. The things that bring in the most traffic to a site like this are per-page descriptions and titles, canonical tags, real headings, image alt text, structured data, modern image formats, and fast loading. These are not things Cargo can do. To get those, the site has to move. To make it easy to move, we already built the new version.

Is the move safe?

We treat a move as a controlled switch, methodical and reversible. The steps that make sure no existing web presence is lost:

01

Inventory every current URL and decide, one by one: keep it, redirect it, or retire it.

02

Preserve the high-value URLs, and build a 301 redirect map for anything whose address changes, so old links and their search value carry forward.

03

Ship the new site with the full search layer already in place: titles, descriptions, canonicals, schema, headings, alt text, and a clean sitemap.

04

Keep the new site on hidden, no-index staging until it passes a page-by-page review.

05

Flip the switch, submit the new sitemap in Search Console, and keep the old links resolving through the redirects.

06

Watch Search Console coverage, rankings, and traffic after launch, and correct anything that moves.

The plan is discipline and measurement throughout the move. A clean switch holds the value KW has earned, and we watch and fix rather than promise no movement. No one honest promises zero turbulence in a migration like this, but we manage any issues excellently.

SMW. We looked at the store the way a search engine does: what we can already see from the outside, what a little read-only access would show, and a simple way to go further.

What kind of store is this?

SMW sells one-of-a-kind pieces. Each is listed once, sells, and is gone, so most product pages are temporary. Search engines give the most weight to pages that stay put, which here means the collection and category pages rather than the individual products.

What can we see now?

We looked only at what is public, the same view search engines have.

01

Sold pieces stay online

More than a quarter of the live product pages are items that already sold but are still online. They cannot be bought, so each should redirect to its collection when it sells, which keeps the search value it earned.

02

Collection pages have no text

Collection pages like Furniture, Lighting, and Vases keep the same address even as the products inside them change, so they are the best place to rank for a search like “vintage brass lamp.” Right now they have no description text for Google to read. This is the biggest opportunity.

03

Titles miss the search words

A page title is a top ranking signal, and the first words count most. Most titles read Name [VINTAGE] – Kaitlyn Wolfe, so the opening goes to the bracket and the brand instead of the words people search: the material, the era, the kind of object.

04

Photos have no alt text

Alt text is the short description attached to a photo that tells Google and screen readers what it shows. Every product image is missing it, so the photos cannot show in Google Images, where much vintage shopping starts.

05

Internal pages are visible in search

A few internal and quote-only collections, meant for staff or private sales, are open to Google alongside the public pages. They should be hidden so the public pages rank on their own.

06

What is already working

Much of the hard part is done. The store loads fast, the technical setup is solid, and it is already wired for the new AI shopping assistants. A strong base to build on.
