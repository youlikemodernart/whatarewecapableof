What Teaspressa's public infrastructure is already showing.

We spent time with Teaspressa's public-facing infrastructure: the DTC store, the wholesale store, the content, the analytics, the DNS records, and the technology connecting them. This is what we found, what it might mean, and what we'd want to learn more about.

Summary

Teaspressa is a Phoenix-based CPG brand. Allison DeVane founded it in 2015, owns 100% of the company, and manufactures the products in-house. The Luxe Mixer Cubes, flavored sugar cubes for cocktails, lattes, and mimosas, are the main product. She appeared on Shark Tank early on, closed physical retail locations during COVID, and pivoted to wholesale. The collabs page states the business generates over seven figures through 13,000+ retail accounts.

Findings

01

The wholesale operation serves 13,000+ retail accounts through a separate Shopify store at wholesale.teaspressa.com. Pricing is login-gated. Products are displayed in case packs. There's a blog coaching retailers on seasonal merchandising, and a "Sip & Shop Demo Package" at $120-$130 for in-store demos. What we didn't find: a digital line sheet, a reorder system, account-level tracking, or a way for a buyer to browse after a trade show without logging into a second e-commerce store.

02

The DTC site runs six analytics and marketing tools: GA4 (G-MY5C4WWCLQ), Hotjar (5041504), Microsoft Clarity (rx17nkfzlk), Facebook Pixel (805449808864249), Klaviyo (UYrTva), and Postscript (7548). Each one drops its own tracking script directly into the page. There's no Google Tag Manager connecting them, no shared data layer, and no unified event model. Six tools collecting data independently means six partial views of the same customer.

03

Shopify's subscription capability is enabled. A "Tea Subscription" collection exists. A subscription page exists. But the subscription page is empty. No product shows "subscribe and save" pricing. No landing page explains what subscribers receive. The seasonal flavor rotation would be a natural subscription driver: new flavors first, before they sell out.

04

The TikTok account has 475 published videos and 5,181 followers. That's roughly 11 followers per video. The product is visually strong: a sugar cube dissolving into a cocktail photographs and films well. The volume suggests real production effort. The follower-to-content ratio suggests the content strategy, hook structure, or targeting isn't connecting with the platform's audience.

05

The Wedding Shop page describes custom Champagne Kits and ends with a contact form. The corporate gifting page follows a similar pattern. Both are customization-driven verticals with larger order values and repeat buyers: corporate accounts reorder annually; brides refer other brides. Both currently require manual quoting for every inquiry.

What's working

The product merchandising is sophisticated: 100+ collections organized by drink type, format, flavor profile, base spirit, caffeine level, occasion, and price point. Seasonal rotation keeps the catalog fresh. Product-specific landing page subdomains, including champagnekit.teaspressa.com, mimosakit.teaspressa.com, and drink.teaspressa.com, suggest campaign-level marketing with QR codes on packaging. The wholesale blog coaching retailers on seasonal prep is a differentiator most CPG brands at this scale don't offer. Premium partnerships include NetJets x Monaco Grand Prix and a Greenwood Brewery collaboration.

What we'd want to learn

These observations come from the outside. We can see the storefront, the source code, the DNS records, and the public content. We can't see the GA4 dashboard, the Klaviyo engagement data, the wholesale account activity, or the trade show follow-up process. The patterns above are specific enough to be useful, and general enough that the real picture could look different once those internal layers are visible. A conversation with Allison would sharpen them considerably.

Research

Company

Teaspressa was founded in January 2015 in Phoenix, AZ. Allison DeVane is the sole owner. She invented a patented tea process that brews like espresso, the origin product, then created the Luxe Mixer Cube format, which became the primary revenue driver. Products are manufactured in-house in Phoenix. The about page describes her as an inventor and scientist. She is a Tory Burch Fellow and a Titan 100 CEO.

The origin story: selling tea from a laundry cart in downtown Phoenix, landing a Shark Tank appearance shortly after, opening a retail store, closing retail during COVID, and pivoting to wholesale-first distribution. The collabs page states the business has grown to over seven figures with 13,000+ retailers.

Subdomains

SSL certificate analysis (crt.sh) reveals six subdomains: teaspressa.com, www, wholesale.teaspressa.com (B2B store), account.teaspressa.com (customer accounts), champagnekit.teaspressa.com, mimosakit.teaspressa.com, and drink.teaspressa.com. The last three are product-specific landing pages, likely used for QR codes on packaging or social campaign targeting.

Structured data

JSON-LD Organization schema is present site-wide with social links; four of eight sameAs entries are empty strings. Product schema is present on product pages with brand, category, GTIN, and offers. The Organization schema uses https://schema.org. The Product schema uses http://schema.org. Both are valid, but the protocols are inconsistent.

What we don't know

Instagram engagement rate, because follower count is 31K, but we can't see per-post engagement without an account. Percentage of the 13,000 accounts that are active in a given year. Annual trade show budget and number of shows. Current follow-up process after a show. Whether the subscription model was tried and abandoned, or never launched. Who manages Shopify day-to-day. Whether the dual review platform situation is a migration in progress. GA4 data on traffic, conversion, and channel performance. Whether Teaspressa is listed on Faire or other wholesale marketplaces. Packaging breakage rate in shipping.

Scope

Where the patterns point

The observations in this report come from publicly visible data. They suggest specific areas worth exploring, but the right priorities depend on context we don't have: internal metrics, trade show economics, team capacity, and what Allison is already planning. What follows is where the patterns point, not a prescription.

Area 1

Wholesale buyer tools

13,000 retail accounts ordering through a login-gated Shopify store. If even a fraction of those accounts are lapsed or underactive, the simplest leverage might be making it easier to reorder: a digital line sheet that a buyer can browse on a phone after a trade show, seasonal collection previews, and a follow-up path that doesn't require navigating a separate e-commerce store. Whether that's the right priority depends on what the wholesale account data shows: how many accounts are active, what the reorder rate looks like, and how follow-up works after a show.

Area 2

Recurring revenue from consumables

The subscription infrastructure is partially built. Shopify has it enabled. A collection and a page exist. If Allison tried subscriptions before and moved on, there's probably a reason. If she hasn't tried them, the seasonal rotation creates a natural subscription story: new flavors before they hit shelves. The wedding and corporate verticals could also benefit from less friction in the quoting process. Whether any of this is worth building depends on the DTC reorder rate and how much of the current revenue these verticals represent.

Area 3

Connected measurement

Six analytics and marketing tools running independently. No container connecting them. DNS records showing signs of accumulation over time. These are the kinds of patterns that make it hard to answer "what's working" with confidence. A unified tracking layer, Google Tag Manager, shared data layer, and consolidated events, would give whoever manages marketing a single view of customer behavior. Whether that's urgent depends on how much the team currently relies on data for decisions versus intuition and relationship.

The first conversation

We'd want to understand the wholesale operation from the inside: how buyers currently find and reorder products, what happens after a trade show, what the active-account percentage looks like, and where Allison feels the friction. We'd want to see the GA4 and Klaviyo data to understand DTC traffic, conversion, and retention patterns. And we'd want to know what she's already tried and what she's planning, so anything we suggest builds on momentum rather than cutting across it.

The research here is the starting point. A conversation with Allison would turn these observations into a clearer picture of where the highest-leverage work sits.
