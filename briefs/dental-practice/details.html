How a practice is organized, and where AI fits

The full breakdown behind the summary: the team, the org chart, and what AI can take from each role. General dentistry, a new independent practice.

Three lanes

Every general practice divides into three lanes, and the line between them is what the law lets you delegate. That line is also the automation line.

  • Clinical. The chair work. Licensed, and delegated down a ladder (dentist, then hygienist, then assistant). Diagnosis and any irreversible treatment are yours alone.
  • Front office. Phones, schedule, insurance, billing, treatment presentation, marketing. Almost none of it requires a license, so almost all of it is open to automation.
  • Management. The office manager plus you wearing the owner hat: hiring, payroll, compliance, supply, the numbers.

The team, role by role

Clinical

  • Dentist / owner. Exam, diagnosis, the treatment plan, and all treatment. Yours by law: diagnosis, planning, prescribing, and anything that cuts tissue.
  • Associate dentist. Same clinical scope, no ownership. Added once one dentist fills the schedule. Same legal boundary as the owner.
  • Hygienist. Cleanings, scaling, perio, x-rays, and the recall engine. Works under your supervision; cannot diagnose or treatment-plan.
  • Expanded-function assistant (EFDA). Places and finishes fillings after you prep, plus sealants and impressions. Reversible work only; scope varies by state.
  • Dental assistant. Four-handed chairside, sterilization, x-rays, temporaries. The least-regulated clinical role; cannot place restorations or diagnose.
  • Sterilization tech. Instrument processing and logs. Often folded into the assistant role early on. No license required.

Front office

  • Front desk / patient coordinator. Greeting, phones, booking, intake, insurance, copays. At launch, one person wears every admin hat.
  • Scheduling coordinator. Owns the book: fills gaps, manages no-shows.
  • Treatment coordinator. Presents the plan, explains financing, converts the consult into a scheduled case.
  • Insurance / billing coordinator. Verification, claims, posting, follow-up on aging accounts.
  • Marketing coordinator. Reviews, referrals, new-patient flow. Usually outsourced or owner-run early.

Management

  • Office manager. Runs the business: hiring, payroll, supply, compliance, the KPIs. Often not a separate hire at open; you or the lead front-desk person covers it.

What sets the exact line

Two things decide precisely where automation stops for your practice.

  • Your state. The non-delegable core is the same everywhere: only a dentist diagnoses, plans, prescribes, or does irreversible work. What varies by state is the rest, and it moves the line: what an expanded-function assistant may do, whether a hygienist can give local anesthesia and under what supervision, and who may take x-rays. We pull your state's rules before setting anything up.
  • Patient privacy. Anything that touches real patient records has to run on privacy-compliant systems that will sign a business-associate agreement. Ordinary consumer AI tools will not. That is exactly why the agent-team idea starts as a simulation on made-up data.

The order to do it in

For a new practice, sequence beats scope. A full schedule is the year-three problem; an empty one is the year-one problem.

  • Before you open. Pick the practice-management software, then stand up scheduling, patient messaging, reviews, and insurance verification.
  • Year one: fill chairs. Never miss a call, book online, confirm, win back anyone who lapses, and gather reviews. This is the revenue lever while the schedule has room.
  • Once you have volume. Add the back-office automation (claims, aging accounts) and the clinical-paperwork tools (note capture, x-ray flagging).
  • Later. Deeper clinical assistance, each tool earning more independence only after it has proven itself on low-risk work first.

The agent-team version

The most ambitious form of this replaces a stack of separate tools with a single coordinated team of AI agents that mirrors the org chart you would otherwise hire.

              OWNER-DENTIST (you, final say)
                        |
            OFFICE-MANAGER AGENT
     (coordinates the team, surfaces the numbers,
      asks you before anything leaves the building)
                        |
   ---------------------+----------------------
   |        |        |         |        |
 Reception Insurance Billing   Recall   Reviews
 & booking                              & marketing
                    |
          Treatment-coordination agent
        (preps the case; you close it)

Two ways to run it, and the order is not optional:

  • Step one: a simulation. We build the whole team on made-up patients and let it run, so you can watch your practice's operation work before you have hired anyone. It runs on ordinary infrastructure because no real patient data is involved, and it doubles as the blueprint for the real thing.
  • Step two, if you want it: the real thing. A version that touches live patient records has to move onto privacy-compliant infrastructure and connect to your real software. It is a bigger build, and for a single new practice, buying proven tools first is usually the smarter first move. The agent team is most powerful if it becomes a repeatable model across practices.

Either way, every agent starts by drafting and asking for your approval, and earns more independence only as it proves out, beginning with the reversible, low-stakes work.
