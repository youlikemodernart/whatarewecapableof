What we found: a first look at Unitus email and SMS

The short version: both channels are real and making money, the text program is in good shape, and the email side has one clear problem worth fixing before the summer sends go out.

The core is healthy

Over the last 30 days, email brought in about $23,000 in attributed revenue and text brought in about $36,000. The text program is especially well run. It has around 18,000 subscribers, it handles the compliance details carefully (quiet hours, limits on how often anyone is messaged, and screening of risky numbers), and most of its revenue comes from automated journeys rather than one-off blasts. That is what a healthy program looks like.

The email list is large, tired, and over-mailed

This is the thing that stands out. The email list has about 73,000 subscribers, but most of them have stopped paying attention. Close to 89,000 contacts are tagged as low-engagement, and the open rate is around 6 percent. Meanwhile the program is sending a lot: roughly 770,000 emails in the last 30 days, which is about ten per subscriber per month.

Heavy mailing to a list that has mostly stopped opening is the pattern that quietly damages an email program. Inbox providers like Gmail watch how people react to your mail. When most of the list ignores it, they start treating all of it as less wanted, and even the good emails slip into spam or the promotions tab. Past a point, more volume works against the sends that actually matter.

This is timely, because the summer calendar is busy: a Back-to-School stretch and an anniversary sale, with a lot of sends close together. Running that on top of a tired list is the moment most likely to push deliverability the wrong way, right when the sales need the inbox.

A few good things are switched off

Looking through the automations, several built-and-ready pieces are sitting idle:

  • Win-back, the flow that re-earns customers who have gone quiet, is off on both email and text. That is revenue left on the table.
  • Post-purchase follow-ups, a VIP track, and birthday messages are built in Mailchimp but were never turned on.
  • The browse-abandonment flow is partly paused.

The welcome and cart-recovery flows are on and working on both channels, so the foundation is solid.

What we would suggest, in order

  1. Health first. Before adding sends, tighten the list: keep mailing the people who open and click, ease well off the people who have gone silent, and run one clean "do you still want to hear from us" pass before letting the rest go. This lifts open rates and protects the inbox, which makes every send after it land better.
  2. Turn the easy wins back on. Revive win-back on both channels, and switch on the post-purchase, VIP, and birthday flows that are already built.
  3. Then run the summer calendar, aimed at the people most likely to buy, with sensible limits on how often any one person hears from you.

None of this is unusual. A list this size naturally collects people who drift away, and the fix is straightforward. The upside is real: a cleaner list and a couple of revived automations usually do more for revenue than another month of heavier sending.
