The Wedding Officiant Kit: a launchable v1

You have enough drafted to launch. The strongest first move is to narrow v1 to the Ceremony Builder, a free lead magnet, and an officiant-first SEO hub, and to hold the AI assistant and the coaching call as the roadmap.

Where things stand

The project is pre-launch: no live site, checkout, or email list yet, a strong core idea, and more launch surfaces than one v1 needs.

The spine is consistent across every doc, and it is the part to build on: the officiant is a storyteller with three stories to tell, the couple's, the community's, and the one ahead. Around that you have a drafted Ceremony Builder spec, a lead magnet of 14 field-tested tips, a welcome and intro sequence, a Christian order of service, an all-audience readings list, Christian and secular meditations, vows coaching, and an officiant checklist.

Two things are unresolved. The full kit is large, around 110 pieces, with roughly a quarter ready to repackage and most still to write or polish. And the plan has drifted: the latest version describes three tiers (self-serve kit, an AI assistant, a 45-minute call), while the earlier build docs point to a narrower Ceremony Builder v1 with AI and coaching deferred. The main thing to solve is scope. Tool choice matters less than deciding what v1 is.

This brief is our read from the materials you shared and current public research on the tools and the officiant market. It is a read of where things stand, not a proposal or a quote.

The recommendation

Launch the Ceremony Builder first, with the free lead magnet and an officiant-first SEO hub. Hold the AI assistant and the call tier as the roadmap.

The Ceremony Builder is the most saleable and least fragile piece you have. Someone asked to officiate a friend's wedding wants a ceremony they can deliver, rather than a 110-piece library to navigate. The builder already has a workable shape in your docs: six essential elements, four optional add-ons, Christian and secular editions, three choices per element, priced around $29 to $39. The 14-tip lead magnet is the free top of the funnel. SEO is the low-budget way people will find it, which is why it belongs in v1 rather than a later phase.

Why this order

Sell the outcome the buyer needs, a ceremony they can deliver, rather than the size of the library. The library is mostly unfinished; the builder is close to sellable. Starting with the builder lets checkout, delivery, and acquisition get proven on the simplest version of the product.

The AI assistant and a coaching call each add operating load before there is proof anyone will buy: support, cost control, and content and safety decisions for the assistant; scheduling, payment, and your time for the call. Launch narrow, prove demand, and those tiers become upgrades you add on purpose instead of obligations you carry from day one.

The three-tier read

The recommendation above is the what. The how has three versions. They differ mostly by who maintains the site and whether this stays your solo product or becomes a system built together.

Tier 1: your current stack

Gumroad for checkout, Carrd or Framer for the site, Kit or beehiiv for email, Calendly or SavvyCal plus Zoom for calls, an AI API for the assistant, Canva or Figma for design. It is a coherent plan for a nontechnical founder and the fastest path to a live page.

Two things to watch. Gumroad is the quickest checkout to stand up, but it takes about 10% plus $0.50 a sale, which is real drag at $29 to $39. And Carrd works as a landing page but not as a content hub, so it will not carry SEO on its own. Honoring the stack, the tightening moves are: use Framer instead of Carrd if SEO matters, start on Kit's free tier, run Calendly free with one call type, and hold the AI tier. Choose this if you want to build and run everything yourself and want the shortest path to live.

Tier 2: conservative, fee-conscious

Same no-code posture, fewer accounts, lower fees. Framer Basic or a small static site for editable SEO pages; Lemon Squeezy or Payhip for checkout, which cut the per-sale fee against Gumroad while still handling delivery, with Lemon Squeezy acting as merchant of record so it takes on more of the tax side; MailerLite free to start the list; Tally free for the questionnaire and lead-magnet capture; Cal.com or Calendly free for one manual call type; the AI assistant as a waitlist rather than a paid tier. Choose this if you own the ongoing maintenance and What are we capable of? stays advisory or does light implementation.

Tier 3: clean-slate and packageable

A small content-first product system instead of a stack assembled from many accounts. A static site (Astro or Next.js) where the ceremony modules, scripts, readings, and pages live as versioned content, with checkout through Lemon Squeezy or Stripe. The builder can start as structured static pages and become interactive later without changing platforms. SEO becomes the site's architecture rather than an add-on, and the same system can be packaged for other expertise-led products. Stripe is the lowest-fee checkout but leaves delivery, tax, and support with whoever runs it. This tier costs more up front and needs a technical owner. Choose this if this is a build-together project rather than a stack pick. It is the cleanest long-term home for the product.

The tool prices and terms above are current public figures. Confirm them before committing to any tool.

What SEO changes

If SEO is the main way people find this on a lean budget, the site cannot be a single Carrd page. It has to be a content hub with crawlable pages for the specific things a first-time officiant searches when the wedding is close and they have never done this.

The broad terms (wedding vows, wedding readings, how to officiate a wedding) are held by big players: wedding publishers, ordination organizations, and existing ceremony builders. Public pages confirm that Universal Life Church runs a script hub and generator, and Provenance has a full ceremony builder. Opening on those head terms is a losing fight.

The opening is the long-tail panic: first-time officiant scripts, a friend officiating a wedding, ceremony run-of-show, day-of checklists, what to say for a friend. Treat that as a direction to validate with live keyword checking before committing the page list; the search volumes are not confirmed yet.

The moat is operational completeness. Free scripts solve inspiration. Your paid product can solve assembly and ceremony-day execution: where to stand, when readings happen, how to cue the rings, what to print, what to say before the processional, what to do at rehearsal. That is the content strategy the site has to serve, and it is why the site choice points to Framer with a CMS or a small static content site over a one-page builder.

What waits

The AI assistant stays out of the paid v1. It can run as a waitlist ("AI ceremony coach coming next"), as a manual concierge where you or What are we capable of? use AI to help draft with a human reviewing, or as a small beta once the static builder is selling. The deferral is about sequencing. A paid AI tier needs its own spec first: what content it is allowed to use, how it handles readings, vows, and religious language, whether it ever touches legal or marriage-license questions or only points to official sources, the cost per session, and the support and refund policy when an answer is wrong. Sell the builder first, then design the assistant against real usage.

The coaching call stays invite-only or a manual follow-up rather than a required tier. A standing call tier becomes a weekly time commitment and adds scheduling and payment steps before checkout and delivery are proven. It can return as an upsell once the product sells on its own.

Next decisions

  1. Confirm v1 scope. The recommendation is Ceremony Builder v1 with the lead magnet and SEO hub, and the full kit, AI, and call tier as the roadmap.
  2. Decide who owns maintenance. If you self-maintain, Tier 1 or Tier 2. If it gets built as a system, Tier 3. That choice picks the stack.
  3. Agree that the AI assistant and the call tier are roadmap, waitlist, or manual for launch.
  4. Point us to any past ceremonies, surveys, and the Storyteller Conversation Guide if they exist; some of the source the docs reference was not in the shared folder, and it would speed up finishing the content.
  5. Before anything ships, plan a copyright and legal-framing pass on readings, marriage-license references, and state rules, written to point at official sources rather than give legal advice.

Nothing here commits a scope or a price. The next step is your call on v1, and the rest of the plan follows from it.
