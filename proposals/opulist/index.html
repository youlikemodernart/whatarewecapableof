What are we capable of?
Prepared for Opulist / April 2026

We spent time with Opulist's public infrastructure: the site's source code, the database structure, the blog tags, the sitemap, and what the analytics are already tracking. This is what we found, what the platform contains that most visitors never see, and where the patterns point.

Summary is what we noticed. Research is the evidence behind each observation and the methodology that produced it. Scope is where we see potential space, and the questions we'd want to explore together.

Summary Research Scope ?

Opulist curates venues by how they feel. The selection criteria are stated on the site: light, coherence, energy, "overall good, sparkly feeling vibe." Sara calls herself Head Aesthetics Curator. The pinned Instagram post asks the question that justifies the platform's existence: "Ever wish there was a site like Yelp but only for aesthetic places?"

The archive is larger than the site surface shows. The sitemap contains 1,708 unique blog tags. What's working

107K Instagram followers in roughly three years, built around a single editorial voice. A searchable database of nearly 2,000 locations. A blog with a deep and growing archive. Affiliate infrastructure. Brand partnerships. Three months of building before the domain was registered. The curation criteria read like design principles: "gorgeous design, inspiring energy, ideal lighting, photogenic but 'plz don't call it instagrammable.'" The consistency of taste across nearly 2,000 listings is the product. Most curation platforms dilute as they scale. This one hasn't.

Company

The Instagram account has grown to 107K followers. She spent three months building before the domain was registered, which tells you something about how the operation runs: substance first, visibility second.

The curation criteria function as editorial principles rather than ranking metrics. "Gorgeous design, inspiring energy, ideal lighting" reads like a brief to a photographer, not a filter on a directory. That consistency across 2,000 listings is the hard part. Most curation platforms lose it well before that scale.

The dataset as an asset

The typical framing for a platform like Opulist is "creator brand with a database." That undersells it. The components, taken separately: a taste-filtered dataset of 2,000 venues across 30+ US cities with no equivalent anywhere; an audience that self-selected for design sensibility; editorial content already testing categories and geographies the product doesn't cover yet; and monetization infrastructure that works but isn't connected to most of what's been built.

Some of that value may not be consumer-facing at all. Concierge services, luxury travel agencies, corporate travel planners, wedding coordinators: they all need curated venue data filtered by something more specific than star ratings, and no one sells it. The database has a second market that hasn't been entered.

The "Neuroaesthetics" framing, which pulled 72K views on TikTok, is a positioning framework no one else owns. "Why certain spaces feel right" is a question hospitality brands spend real money to answer.

The observations in this report come from publicly visible data. They suggest specific areas worth exploring, but the right priorities depend on context we don't have: internal metrics, revenue breakdown, team capacity, and what's already planned. What follows is where the patterns point.

Area 1

Compliance and structured data

Two items visible from the outside that could be addressed quickly. The structured data marks the site as a LocalBusiness; the correct Schema.org types for a directory are WebSite or ItemList. This is the signal sent to search engines about what the site fundamentally is, and it takes about 15 minutes to correct in Squarespace's SEO settings. Separately, brand feature posts don't carry visible FTC disclosure language. Whether disclosures exist elsewhere (in agreements, in story posts) isn't visible from the outside, but the blog posts themselves don't include them.

Area 2

Revenue infrastructure across content types

Affiliate integration is active in one content format. Nine content types exist. Hotel listings receive traffic but don't link to booking platforms; those links alone could generate recurring revenue from traffic the site already receives. The newsletter runs on a platform that can't segment by city or interest, which limits both the subscriber experience and the sponsorship value. Whether expanding affiliate coverage or migrating the newsletter is the higher-leverage move depends on what the traffic and revenue data show internally.

Area 3

The B2B dataset

A taste-filtered venue dataset of this size and specificity has no equivalent. The consumer-facing product is the obvious use. The less obvious use: licensing curated venue data to concierge services, luxury travel agencies, corporate travel planners, and wedding coordinators. These are buyers who need something more specific than star ratings and can't build the dataset themselves. Whether this market is worth pursuing depends on the cost of maintaining and packaging the data, and whether the editorial filter can be preserved at scale.

Area 4

Scaling the operation

The constraint on all of the above is the same thing that gave the platform its integrity: everything runs through one editorial voice. That's a phase. The question is which parts of the operation can be systematized or delegated to a vetted contributor network without losing the filter. The curator stays Head Aesthetics Curator. The operation grows around the editorial voice instead of through it. Whether this is the right moment to explore that depends on capacity, ambition, and what the internal workload actually looks like.
