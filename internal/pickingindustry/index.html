Picking Industry

Overview

Industry
AI-powered business operations for founder-led companies
Working definition
What are we capable of? starts as a fractional AI CTO and implementation practice for founder-led organizations whose work is trapped across people, tools, documents, and habits. The productized version is an operating graph plus agent layer that maps the work, recommends build, buy, wait, or retire, and runs the parts that earn their place.

Category Score Notes / Evidence
Market Size & Growth 5 The AI Company Explorer lists 17,049 AI companies and $658.8B in total funding. Relevant categories are large: SaaS has 2,098 companies and $109.6B funding; Generative AI has 1,207 companies and $174.4B; Enterprise Software has 719 companies and $34.6B; Business Intelligence has 503 companies and $10.5B; Business Process Automation has 65 companies and $5.7B; RPA has 107 companies and $6.1B. A category union around consulting, enterprise software, SaaS, productivity, business intelligence, data integration, knowledge management, BPA, and RPA includes 4,242 companies and $151.1B. This is not TAM, but it shows investor and founder attention.
Revenue 4 Near-term revenue can come from high-ticket audits, implementation projects, retainers, and advisory packages. A few clients at $8K to $25K projects or $3K to $10K monthly retainers can support the practice. Gross margins should be high after the initial research, templates, and systems are reusable. The open question is whether this remains a services practice or turns into repeatable software.
Customer Problems 5 The customer is a founder or operator whose company still runs through a few people. Daily pain: scattered knowledge, messy handoffs, too many tools, unclear decisions, manual reporting, no AI owner, and fear of automating the wrong thing. The public site already names the problem: in a founder-led company, decisions, exceptions, and tacit work often run through one person. Tend is targeting the same bottleneck: $500K to $5M revenue, 2 to 15 people, 6 to 12 tools, every decision still flowing through the founder.
Dominating the Industry 3 The first wedge can lead to more: map one workflow, build the first useful system, then expand across reporting, customer follow-up, internal knowledge, finance, operations, sales, and decision support. The risk is that a general AI consulting practice does not dominate anything. Domination requires picking a narrow customer segment or workflow, accumulating reusable patterns, and turning engagements into a productized operating graph.
Urgency & Longevity 5 Urgency is high because AI adoption pressure is immediate and confusing. Founders are being pushed toward ChatGPT, Zapier, agents, AI agencies, and platform tools without a clear decision model. Longevity is strong because the underlying problem is organizational: how work, judgment, data, and tools fit together. The tools will change; the need for owned systems and better decisions will remain.
Why now? 5 Three things changed in the last 2 to 3 years: LLMs became usable on messy business text, agent tooling can now read and act across SaaS tools, and founders now feel pressure to make an AI plan before they have an AI executive. APIs, connectors, browser automation, MCP-style tool access, and better models make small teams able to build useful systems that required larger engineering teams before.
Stay Out of Jail / Make Money / Save Money Impact 4 The core buyer driver is save money and make money: less manual work, cleaner follow-up, faster decisions, better customer service, and fewer dropped opportunities. It can also reduce risk when systems include approvals, audit trails, ownership, and clear failure paths. Compliance is a secondary wedge for regulated clients, not the first claim.
Customer Willingness to Pay 4 Buyers already spend on consultants, agencies, automation tools, VAs, operations staff, data contractors, and SaaS subscriptions. Tend's copy is useful evidence that this buyer recognizes the cost of a missing operator or unaffordable COO. What are we capable of? needs to quantify ROI in customer terms: hours saved per week, faster cycle time, fewer missed renewals, fewer manual handoffs, better lead response, lower error rates.
Problem Complexity 5 These are systemic problems, not one-off tasks. The best work sits across tools, people, incentives, data quality, approvals, and business judgment. That favors the firm's strength because it sells judgment before tooling. It also means scoping discipline matters.
Ease of Access 4 Austin has founder, faith, creative, retreat, and relationship-driven access. Noah has proof-of-insight artifacts, public positioning, and working systems. The first customer motion can be warm referrals, events, short diagnostic conversations, and proof-of-insight pages. Cold outbound is possible, but category education will be harder than warm trust.
Sales Cycle & Buyer Behavior 3 Founder-led SMB sales can close faster than enterprise if the pain is obvious and the ask is concrete. The buyer is usually the founder or operator, with fewer committees. The risk is ambiguity: AI projects can sound expensive, undefined, or risky. GTM should avoid broad AI transformation language and sell a narrow first outcome. Referrals and events should work better than ads early.
Enterprise Upside Potential 4 Once one workflow works, expansion is natural: new departments, more integrations, more approval paths, more decision support. In enterprise, this becomes governance, knowledge management, workflow automation, and agent operations. The enterprise upside exists, but the likely first wedge is founder-led teams where trust and speed matter more than procurement.
Entry Status 5 Best entry path: observe workflows, interview the founder/operator, map tools, identify one bottleneck, and ship one system. What are we capable of? can enter as an AI agency, fractional AI CTO, lunch interview, event follow-up, referral, or proof-of-insight. The current site and manifesto already define the method: map, decide, build, hand off.
Personal Edge / Unique Advantage 5 Noah's edge is systems thinking, AI implementation, agent harnesses, workflow mapping, writing, and translating messy work into working tools. Austin's edge is network, sales, energy, and founder access. The philosophical edge is build, buy, wait, or retire. A product company like Tend cannot honestly say the answer might be do not build on our platform.
Conviction & Passion 5 The internal manifesto, public site, and Austin/Noah conversation all point at the same obsession: organizations becoming capable of more than one person can hold. This is a 10-year problem if the team keeps it tied to real operating work instead of AI theater.
Team Preference 5 Strong fit. Noah can map, build, document, and make the work legible. Austin can sell, source, read people, and carry the energy into founder rooms. The pairing works best if roles stay clear: Austin creates access and client context; Noah turns the mess into systems, language, and delivery; both decide the offer and standards.
Comparable Successes 4 Useful comps from AI Company Explorer: UiPath ($1.99B funding) and Automation Anywhere ($1.05B) show the scale of business process automation; WorkFusion ($386M) uses AI agents for business process automation; Aisera ($164M) sells multi-agent enterprise workflows; Accrete AI ($79M) sells decision automation and knowledge engines; Airia ($50M) sells enterprise AI agents and workflow automation; Bardeen ($21.8M) sells no-code workflow automation; Agency AI and Powered_by Agency show an AI agency and bespoke agent-solutions version. Tend is the closest public articulation to the offer.
Competition Landscape 3 The space is crowded: RPA incumbents, AI agent startups, workflow tools, AI agencies, consultants, no-code platforms, and vertical SaaS companies. The opportunity is not being another generic AI agency. The opening is a narrow, trusted, founder-led positioning with accountable recommendations, implementation, documentation, and an honest refusal to build when building is wrong.
Defensibility 3 Generic consulting has weak defensibility. Defensibility improves if What are we capable of? builds proprietary workflow maps, reusable system templates, client-specific operating graphs, evaluation harnesses, documentation standards, and trusted relationships. Data and integrations can compound inside a client. Brand and trust can compound across a niche.
Short-Term Wins 5 Clear first wins: map a founder's operating graph, automate one reporting or follow-up workflow, clean a messy intake process, build a lead/proposal system, create a client source ledger, route email or form inputs, or build a small AI assistant around a specific company knowledge base. The first win should be narrow, measurable, and useful within 2 to 4 weeks.
Niche Depth 4 Good niche depth if What are we capable of? chooses deliberately. Options include founder-led creative businesses, small professional services firms, nonprofits, schools, commerce businesses, real estate operators, and faith/retreat networks. Each has repeated workflows and trust-based buyers. The next decision is which segment gets the first repeatable package.
Long-Term Passion / Motivation 5 Strong. This combines AI, organizational design, systems, founder psychology, writing, and implementation. It stays interesting because every client has a different operating graph, but the underlying method can become repeatable.
VC narrative strength 4 The VC story is strong if framed as an AI operations layer for founder-led businesses or a productized operating graph that agents can use. The funding data supports investor interest in adjacent categories. The narrative weakens if What are we capable of? stays a bespoke consulting firm with no repeatable software, data asset, or narrow market thesis.
Capital efficiency 5 Very capital efficient as a services-first wedge. The team can sell expertise, fund tooling from projects, and turn repeated patterns into product over time. The largest early cost is founder time, not infrastructure. Heavy burn is unnecessary until a product direction has evidence.
Biggest Challenges with the industry 3 Challenges: noisy market, buyer skepticism, security and privacy concerns, liability from bad automation, unclear ROI, bespoke scope creep, hard positioning, and pressure to sound like every other AI agency. The antidote is a narrow first wedge, proof before scale, measurable outcomes, and the firm's refusal rules: no AI theater, no deceptive automation, no surveillance-first systems, no unowned deployments.

Bottom line

This is a strong industry for What are we capable of? if the first wedge stays narrow: founder-led companies with real operating pain, no AI owner, and work trapped across people and tools. The strongest version is not "AI consulting" in general. It is executive AI judgment plus implementation: map the work, decide what should change, build only what earns its place, and leave the system with the team.
