Rammed-earth labor estimate brief for Otto

A feasibility read on whether a two-month, no-overtime field plan can hold for the library rammed-earth scope, using public wage sources and a drawing-based advisory takeoff.

Prepared by What are we capable of? | Sanitized brief | Raw bid-set material, source packets, and wage-source dumps are intentionally excluded.

Bottom line

The two-month plan is plausible for the library-only base scope, but the margin is narrow. The planning scenario reaches about 35 field workdays, leaving about 5 workdays of slack inside a 40-workday screening window. The risk scenario reaches about 47 field workdays and misses the window.

The answer turns on scope discipline and production assumptions: the 6 CY/day rate must hold for wall mass, and the work around forms, reinforcement, embeds, openings, bevels, staging, testing, inspection, and cleanup must stay close to the planning allowance.

Use boundary: this page is an advisory feasibility brief. Do not use it as a bid, final price, validated takeoff, engineering opinion, wage-compliance statement, or instruction to perform work.

Quantity basis

The advisory takeoff reads the library rammed-earth footprint at about 210 square feet in plan, equivalent to about 90 linear feet of 28 inch wall. The base gross/control quantity range is about 90 to 126 cubic yards, with a planning midpoint around 105 to 115 cubic yards before segment-level opening netting.

An expert drawing review found one material correction: the high-roof plus below-grade stress case can reach about 134 cubic yards if that condition applies across the full measured footprint. The 126 CY risk proxy should stay in the scenario table for comparability, but it should not be treated as a cap.

Summary figures: about 210 SF measured rammed-earth plan footprint, 90 to 126 CY base gross/control range before segment-level netting, and about 134 CY for the high-roof plus below-grade stress case flagged by review.

Openings and scope exclusions

Above-floor scheduled openings create a material sensitivity of about 17 CY if all of those openings belong to applicable rammed-earth wall segments and were not already netted. That sensitivity can reduce material, but it should not reduce field duration without a marked-up segment takeoff because openings also add formwork, bucks, boards, bevels, head and jamb work, irregular geometry, and coordination.

Floor-level doors and openings should not be deducted mechanically because they appear likely to be plan-level gaps already. A pricing-ready quantity needs a segment-level elevation markup before replacing the current gross/control range.

The amphitheater remains excluded from the base model and should be carried as a separate clarification or alternate until the scope conflict is resolved.

Schedule logic

At 6 CY/day, the base gross/control library mass implies about 15 to 21 ramming production days. The 134 CY stress case implies about 22 to 23 ramming days. Those days cover wall mass only.

The planning scenario uses 19 mass-production days, 12 days for non-mass field work, and 4 days for mobilization, layout, QC, and punch. That totals 35 field workdays. A real calendar still needs holidays, weather assumptions, site access, inspection hold points, mockup approval, material lead times, form-cycle logic, and work-date controls.

Wage and classification posture

Public California DIR Southern California wage sources informed the scenario model. The sources support candidate rates for laborer, carpenter, operating engineer, field soils and material tester, teamster, crane or hoisting work, and cement mason work, but rammed earth is not directly named in the reviewed determinations.

The wage model is therefore a cautioned scenario model, not a compliance position. Craft-by-task classification still needs confirmation for wall-mass labor, formwork, reinforcement, equipment operation, trucking or material movement, testing support, inspection support, foreman treatment, and PM treatment. Bid advertisement date, work date, locality, travel, subsistence, apprentice, training, shift, holiday, and contract requirements still matter.

Questions before pricing or external reliance

  1. Does 6 CY/day cover only rammed-earth mass, or also forms, stripping, reinforcement, embeds, bucks, bevels, material handling, testing, inspection, and cleanup?
  2. Are both foremen productive working foremen all day, partly working supervisors, or non-working supervisors?
  3. What PM treatment should be used: overhead, billed management, part-time, full-time, or covered craft time for any work?
  4. Which craft classifications apply to each work bucket under the controlling public-works rules?
  5. What bid advertisement date and work dates control wage determinations and July 2026 increases?
  6. How many formwork sets are available, what length and lift height can each set handle, and what is the strip/reset cycle?
  7. Is the amphitheater excluded, alternate, gabion, or rammed earth in base scope?
  8. Which price components belong in the next model: PM, materials, equipment, testing, insurance, bonds, taxes, travel, overhead, profit, and contingency?

Recommended use

Use the planning scenario as the current assumption-review model: about 35 field workdays and about $128k direct field craft cost before PM and all excluded cost categories. Carry the risk scenario because documented openings, embeds, reinforcement, inspection, and classification uncertainty can push the job past 40 workdays.

The next useful step is a focused clarification pass with Otto: production-rate scope, foreman and PM treatment, craft classifications, self-perform boundaries, calendar window, formwork cycle, and amphitheater scope. An external pricing version should wait until those answers are in hand.
