How a practice is organized, and where AI fits

The full breakdown behind the summary: the team, the org chart, and what AI can take from each role. A small, independent architecture firm.

Three lanes

Every small firm divides into three lanes, and the line between them is what your license and your contracts reserve to you. That line is also the automation line.

  • Design and liability. The licensed core: design authorship, the seal, responsible control over every technical submission, code sign-off, and construction-observation judgment. Yours by law, and the firm's actual product.
  • The operating apparatus. Business development, proposals and fees, project setup, code and permit tracking, consultant coordination, documentation QA, specs, and the construction-admin logs. Almost none of it requires a license, so almost all of it is open to automation.
  • Management. The operating-partner seat plus you wearing the owner hat: the pipeline, the books, staffing, compliance, the numbers.

The team, role by role

Design and liability

  • Principal architect / owner. Design authorship, the client relationship, final judgment, the seal, and the risk. Yours by law: command of the architectural services, the seal, and code sign-off.
  • Project architect. Runs a project's technical development and coordinates the set. Works under the principal's responsible control; often on the licensure track.
  • Job captain / production. Assembles and details the drawing set. Reversible production work; sealed only under a licensed architect's responsible control.

The operating apparatus

  • Business development / marketing. Pipeline, portfolio, outreach, and the proposal content library.
  • Proposal / fee coordinator. Go/no-go, fee and staffing scenarios, assembling the proposal.
  • Project manager / studio coordinator. Schedules, phase-gate checklists, action logs, deliverable tracking.
  • Code / permit coordinator. Zoning and code checklists, the permit matrix per jurisdiction, resubmission tracking.
  • Consultant coordinator. The consultant roster, agreements and insurance on file, deliverable schedules.
  • Documentation / specs / QA. Sheet-index and standards checks, redline and issue logs, spec editing support, product data.
  • Construction-admin coordinator. Submittal, RFI, change-order, payment, and closeout logs, and the response clocks.

Management

  • Operating partner / office manager. Runs the business: pipeline, the books, staffing, compliance, the KPIs. In a small firm this is one seat, often you.
  • Bookkeeper / finance. Timesheets, invoicing, work-in-progress, aged receivables, the monthly close.

What sets the exact line

Two things decide precisely where automation stops for your practice.

  • Your state board. The non-delegable core is the same everywhere: a licensed architect holds responsible control, seals the documents, and signs off on code compliance, and only a licensed person or a registered firm may present the work as architecture at all. What varies by state is the rest, who may own the firm and who must hold command of the services, and it moves the line. We pull your state's rules before setting anything up. None of this is legal or licensure advice; your state board and your counsel govern.
  • Confidentiality and liability. Anything an agent drafts exists for your review and your seal; nothing is sealed, certified, or sent on its own. Client program and financial data, unpublished design work, and anything headed toward a dispute run on private infrastructure, not an ordinary AI tool. That is exactly why the agent-team idea starts as a controlled build on sample material, not a live hookup to your files.

The order to do it in

For a small firm, sequence beats scope. The back office comes before the clever parts.

  • First, the system of record. Stand up the books and the project tracker: timesheets, work-in-progress, invoicing, and the drawing and permit logs. Bounded, checkable, and everything else reads from it.
  • Then the project manager's paperwork. Schedules, phase-gate checklists, action logs, code and consultant tracking.
  • Then business development and proposals. Go/no-go, fee scenarios, the content library, outreach drafts, with every send still yours to approve.
  • When a project reaches construction, the CA logs. The most paperwork-dense phase, and the one where liability concentrates: logs and clocks first, drafting second, professional determinations never.
  • Later, the documentation and specs help, once drawing volume justifies it.

Keep the design and the liability human, and spend the freed time there. The point of the back office is the front of the house: the design, the clients, the judgment.

The agent-team version

The most ambitious form of this replaces a stack of separate tools with a single coordinated team of AI agents that mirrors the office you would otherwise hire.

Two ways to run it, and the order is not optional:

  • Step one: a controlled build. We build the whole team against one representative project and let it run, so you can watch your practice's operation work before it touches anything live. Because it runs on sample material, no confidential client work is exposed, and it doubles as the blueprint for the real thing.
  • Step two, if you want it: the real thing. A version wired to your live files and software runs on private infrastructure and connects to your real tools. It is a bigger build. For a single small practice, buying a few proven tools first is often the smarter first move; the agent team earns its keep as a repeatable operating system, across your own projects or as something the firm keeps for good.

Either way, every agent starts by drafting and asking for your approval, and earns more independence only as it proves out, beginning with the reversible, low-stakes work. And the hard line never moves: no agent signs, seals, certifies, determines code compliance, commits a fee, or sends anything on its own.
