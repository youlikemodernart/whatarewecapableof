Program Transfer Portal Path ?

We spent two weeks with eight full session transcripts from the April 2026 cohort. Sales School is more structured and more deliberate than the email-and-PDF delivery surface suggests. The program alternates between live Zoom sessions and self-paced video breakouts across two days. Jake facilitates the live sessions. Students watch Wistia videos during breaks. The live debriefs after each breakout are where the teaching happens: Jake calls on students by name, listens to their video takeaways, and layers stories and frameworks on top of their responses.

What the transcripts show

01

The emotional architecture is deliberate. Day 1 is mental and emotional: build students up (Session 1: you chose this, the industry is real, curiosity is key), deliver hard truths (the 12 Things You Need to Know About Sales: harder than you think, less money before more, lost sleep, stigma), then rebuild with the Cycle of Achievement, Jake's centerpiece 10-step framework that closes Day 1. Day 2 pivots to technical skills: communication, the approach, objection handling. Session 8 brings both threads together: 30 minutes of tactical Q&A, then the FIRST commitment talk (Future, Initiative, Resilience, Standards, Tenacity), ending with Jake's own origin story told in third person and revealed. The 30-60 day commitment is the throughline from Session 1, minute one.

02

Jake's live teaching is reactive. He has 19 named frameworks and a library of 45+ stories and analogies, each attached to a specific curriculum moment. He deploys them in response to what students say. He calls on raised hands, listens, validates, then adds a teaching layer. In one exchange, he drills a student from "I want savings" down to a specific $499.99 MIDI keyboard, demonstrating goal specificity in real time. In another, he coaches a student through the purpose framework for ten minutes. He never reads from slides.

03

Post-training tracking is manual. Jake contacts partner companies for approximately 90 days after each cohort, asking how students are performing. If a student fails, he considers it his fault. No app-based tracking exists. A prototype called Manus, built by Peter in roughly 5 hours, includes video hosting, a script tester, a Peak Performance Planner, and a 30-day post-field content shell. It was never launched.

04

The content delivery surface adds friction during live breaks. The path from entry to playback crosses seven surfaces: GoHighLevel preview, Firebase email HTML, email index, day section, breakout button, PDF index, Wistia page. For post-session review, the extra steps are minor. During a timed Zoom break with 80 students, they compound.

Jake was explicit about phasing on the call: Austin runs the existing program first, unchanged. Hold metrics for 90 days. Isolate person from system. "Integrate before you innovate." The portal comes after Austin proves he can run the room.

What Austin inherits

The video library, unchanged. The six-breakout structure and its sequence. The major frameworks: Cycle of Achievement, FIRST, 12 Things You Need to Know, 6 Core Values, Four Aces, the Columbo Effect, the Buying Atmosphere, Cycle of the Approach, and 11 others documented in the curriculum map. The exercises and assignments: goals, purpose, vision, 25 Skills self-assessment, Carrot and the Stick, nightly script reading. The two-day rhythm and the alternating live, video, debrief format. The Wistia content. Miranda's logistics support.

What Austin replaces

Jake's personal stories. Jack's story is Jake's origin: a 19-year-old whose car breaks down, who knocks 80 hours for $40 in his first week, finishes #17 in the world as a rookie, comes back 8 more years. "His name wasn't Jack." That reveal is the program's emotional climax, and it only works as autobiography.

Austin needs his own versions of five recurring story types: the opening energy story (Jake uses Gertrude Ederle swimming the English Channel, JJ Spaun at the US Open), the "this work is hard and here is why it is worth it" story (Jake uses the 12 Things as the vehicle), the resilience story (Jake uses David Goggins at length), the commitment story (Jake uses Jason's trajectory from drug dealer to Tony Robbins salesperson, Joe from Jenny Craig who made $37 after 90 days), and the personal origin reveal. The frameworks transfer. The stories that carry conviction are personal.

What Austin develops

The Socratic method Jake uses in every Q&A debrief. Calling on students by name and responding to their specific takeaways in real time with a relevant reframe or story. Emotional arc management: knowing where each session needs to peak, where to push, where to let the room breathe. His own version of the Session 8 commitment talk.

Preparation path

Step 1
Observe

Watch all 8 session recordings. Study the curriculum map and content inventory. Map the emotional arc across both days. Identify which frameworks Austin can teach now and which need development.

Step 2
Shadow

Attend a live cohort as a participant. Study Jake's call-on and response patterns. Note the moments where his teaching pivots from framework to story.

Step 3
Co-facilitate

Run defined segments with Jake in the room. The homework debrief (Session 5) and a breakout debrief are natural starting points: structured enough to prepare for, reactive enough to test the Socratic method.

Step 4
Solo with support

Run a full two-day cohort with Jake available but not visible to students.

Step 5
Solo

The 90-day metrics window begins. Austin runs the program independently and holds the results.

The live-break console

The strongest interface idea is a current-state screen. During Zoom breaks, students need to know what to watch, in what order, and when to return. The Today page shows the active breakout, the watch sequence, and the return time. The facilitator sets the active breakout from a control surface. One link shared in Zoom chat replaces the PDF lookup.

Text version

In Zoom, the facilitator selects the active breakout and cues students. Students open the Today page. The portal shows the active breakout, watch sequence, and return prompt. Students watch the assigned sequence, then return to Zoom for debrief.

Post-training surface

The portal's highest-value extension covers the 90 days after the two-day session. Students need video access for review (Jake says the content makes more sense after field experience), commitment tracking for 30, 60, or 90 days, the Carrot and the Stick exercise, a nightly script reading log, and a channel for check-ins that Jake currently handles manually. Peter's Manus prototype was aimed at this same gap. App-based tracking has the clearest return here: the training is 2 days, but the behavior change happens over 90.

Three phases. Each depends on the one before it.

Phase 1: Facilitation transfer

First

Austin learns to run the room

Observe, shadow, co-facilitate, solo. The 90-day metrics window begins when Austin runs his first independent cohort. Jake defined this phase on the call: prove the system works with a different facilitator before building anything new.

Output: Austin can run Sales School with results comparable to Jake's.

Phase 2: Course portal

Then

Build the delivery platform

Replace the PDF indexes with breakout pages. Add the Today page for live breaks, a facilitator control surface, a post-training tracking layer, and a resources section. Retain Wistia as the media layer. Austin's facilitation experience informs the portal design: running the room reveals what the delivery surface needs to do.

Output: a navigable training portal that supports live breaks, later review, and 90-day follow-up.

Phase 3: Platform layer

If needed

Add scale features

Search, progress analytics, admin editing, cohort management, login, and reporting. These features become useful when the school runs multiple cohorts per year and needs to track students across them.

Output: a reusable learning platform.
