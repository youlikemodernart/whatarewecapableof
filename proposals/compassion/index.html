A Gen Z content system for Compassion Causes.

Compassion International is the 11th-largest charity in the United States. Compassion Causes is their effort to reach the next generation. The sub-brand has the raw material: 27 countries of church partnerships, a parent brand with 258K followers and 950K email subscribers, and a platform that works. What it lacks is a connection to that distribution, a visual identity, and an editorial direction. A content strategy that addresses all three can reach the team's $1M fundraising goal.

Research

Compassion International has $1.3 billion in annual private donations, 258K Instagram followers, 950K email subscribers, and 73 years of church partnerships across 27 countries. Compassion Causes, their Gen Z fundraising platform, has 1,800 Instagram followers and 87 TikTok followers. The main compassion.com homepage does not link to compassioncauses.com.

The team's stated goal is $1M in fundraised donations within a year. The current infrastructure and budget cannot reach it. That gap is where we'd work.

Findings

01

Compassion Causes is architecturally disconnected from its parent brand. Separate domain, separate CMS, separate analytics, separate user accounts. A Compassion donor who sponsors a child cannot use that login on Compassion Causes. The 258K Instagram audience, the 950K email list, and the homepage itself do not drive traffic to Causes. The sub-brand was never connected to its distribution.

02

The content on the platform has no editorial layer. Churches write their own cause descriptions and titles. Some are compelling. One is titled "Enhance Your Baking Skills: Tips and Techniques for Perfect Results." The raw material: pig farming for self-reliance, literacy programs, classroom construction across 27 countries. The packaging is where it breaks down.

03

The influencer campaigns produced poor ROI, per the team's assessment. The causes they drove traffic to use long, unmemorable web addresses, church-written copy, and a design disconnected from the Compassion brand donors trust. An influencer can drive clicks; the page it lands on does not close.

04

The social feed currently mixes several visual treatments without a consistent system: cell phone snapshots, blue infographic cards, talking-head Reels, platform screenshots, and stock-style impact photography. No shared color palette, no content series, no editorial calendar. For comparison: Samaritan's Purse has 350K followers with consistent visual treatment. Compassion's main account has 258K with professional photography. Compassion Causes has 1,800.

05

$1,200 per month against a $1M fundraising goal. At $20 average donation and 2% conversion, $1M requires 2.5 million impressions. $1,200/month does not generate that on any platform. The budget appears to have been set by organizational default, with no line drawn back to the target.

The precedent

K-Love is the largest contemporary Christian music radio network in the world: 600+ stations, 12 million weekly listeners, 1 million Instagram followers. Their core audience skews 25-54. Gen Z does not discover faith content through FM radio.

K-Love's response was Goodsource: a separate brand with 7 hired Gen Z creators, a 4-person creative team, two video podcast shows, and partnerships with campus movements like Unite Us (71K Instagram, thousands of students at events). The footer says "K-Love, Inc." but the brand face is distinct. They invested in people first, then content, then events.

Compassion Causes took the opposite approach: platform first, then an influencer agency. K-Love hired creators to build an audience. Compassion bought impressions from an agency. The structural logic of K-Love's investment validates the direction we'd propose.

Content

The content direction

The Compassion Causes Instagram feed should do three things: make causes feel like stories, let the audience see themselves in the work, and build a visual identity distinct from Compassion International's institutional polish.

The outcomes: a feed with a recognizable visual identity, a content pipeline that produces three posts per week from church partner material, and an evidence base for requesting additional organizational investment.

Four content pillars. Three grid posts per week. Daily Stories when there's material.

The feed today

Content ideas

Each numbered idea below is a repeatable content format: a series that can run weekly or monthly. The formats are designed to be produced by a small team, primarily from church partner submissions with editorial direction from us.

01

One Cause, One Minute

A single cause distilled into 60 seconds. One person at the site explains what they need and what it would change. Phone-shot. Subtitled. Progress bar ending.

Reel / TikTok / Shorts · On the Ground

02

Day-in-the-Life

Follow one church partner through a single day. Morning service through program delivery. First-person narration. The content no competitor can replicate.

Carousel (8-10 slides) or 90s video · On the Ground

03

Before / After

A funded cause in two states. The empty lot and the finished classroom. The broken pipe and the running faucet. Tangible proof that donations did something.

Split-frame post or transition Reel · The Proof

04

The Numbers Card

One statistic, designed with impact. "47 kids now have clean water because 312 people gave an average of $8." Specific, small, real. Not "3,600 children impacted."

Single-image post · The Proof

05

What Would You Build?

Show a community's need. Ask the audience what they'd prioritize. Reveal what the community actually chose and link to the cause. Participation before donation.

Story polls / Reel with comment prompt · The Invite

06

Funded Celebration

When a cause reaches 100%, celebrate it. Quick montage: the page hitting 100%, the community, what comes next. Making completion feel like an event.

Reel (15-30s) · The Proof

Visual identity

01

Photography dominates

Real photos from real places. The blue infographic cards currently on the feed become the exception, not the rule.

02

Warm palette

The parent brand is institutional blue. The sub-brand should be warm: earth tones, golden hour, the colors of the communities served. Visual differentiation from @compassion.

03

Consistent text treatment

One typeface, one style when text appears on images. Serif or hand-drawn, not the current mix of sans-serif infographic treatments.

04

No stock photography

Every image traceable to a real place and a real cause.
