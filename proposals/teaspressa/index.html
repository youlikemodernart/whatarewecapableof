Scope

Teaspressa has done what most CPG founders talk about: built a product from scratch, manufactured it in-house, and gotten it into more than thirteen thousand retail locations without outside investment. That distribution is the asset. It took eleven years of trade shows, Shark Tank visibility, and Allison's personal energy to build.

The question now is whether the systems behind that distribution can support the next phase. We looked at the public-facing infrastructure: the DTC site, the wholesale store, the content, the analytics, and the technology connecting all of it. Five findings stood out.

Five Findings

1. The wholesale operation runs on a basic storefront

Teaspressa serves 13,000+ retail accounts through a separate Shopify store at wholesale.teaspressa.com. Pricing is login-gated, products are displayed in case packs, and there's a blog coaching retailers on seasonal merchandising. That's the right instinct. But there's no line sheet, no reorder system, no account-level tracking, and no way for a buyer to browse after a trade show without logging into a second e-commerce store. For a business that spends significantly on trade show presence, every conversation that doesn't convert to an easy reorder path is lost momentum.

Source: wholesale.teaspressa.com product catalog, login-gated pricing, DNS subdomain analysis
2. The analytics stack isn't connected

The DTC site runs GA4, Hotjar, Microsoft Clarity, Facebook Pixel, Klaviyo, and Postscript. Each one drops its own tracking script directly into the page. There's no Google Tag Manager tying them together, no shared data layer, and no unified event model. The result: six tools, six partial views of customer behavior, and no complete picture. When Allison or her team asks "what's working," each tool gives a different answer because each one sees a different slice of the same visitor.

Source: homepage source code analysis; GA4 property G-MY5C4WWCLQ, Hotjar ID 5041504, Clarity ID rx17nkfzlk, FB Pixel 805449808864249, Klaviyo UYrTva, Postscript 7548
3. The subscription model exists in pieces but not in practice

Shopify's subscription capability is enabled. A "Tea Subscription" collection exists. A subscription page exists. But the page is empty. No product shows "subscribe and save" pricing. No landing page explains what a subscription includes. For a consumable product, this gap means every customer relationship resets to zero after purchase. The seasonal flavor rotation is a natural subscription driver: subscribers get new flavors first, before they sell out. That story hasn't been built yet.

Source: Apple Pay config ("supportsSubscriptions": true), /collections/teaspressa-tea-subscription, /pages/tea-subscribe-page (empty)
4. TikTok production outpaces results by a wide margin

475 published videos. 5,181 followers. That's roughly 11 followers per video. For a product as visually compelling as a sugar cube dissolving into a cocktail, the format should work. The volume suggests significant time and production effort. The low follower-to-content ratio suggests either the content strategy, the hook structure, or the targeting isn't connecting. This might be a content problem, or it might mean TikTok isn't where Teaspressa's core buyer spends time. Either way, the current return on that production effort is low.

Source: TikTok @teaspressa profile metadata (followerCount: 5181, videoCount: 475, heartCount: 19600)
5. The wedding and corporate verticals run through a contact form

The Wedding Shop page describes custom Champagne Kits and ends with "fill out the inquiry form below." The corporate gifting page follows a similar pattern. Both of these should be high-margin customization plays: larger order values, repeat event buyers, corporate accounts that reorder annually. Instead, every inquiry requires manual quoting. A bride comparing options will choose the vendor who gives her a price in 30 seconds over the one that asks her to fill out a form and wait.

Source: /pages/the-wedding-shop, /pages/corporate-gifting
What's working well: The product merchandising is sophisticated. 100+ collections organized by drink type, format, flavor profile, base spirit, caffeine level, occasion, and price point. The seasonal rotation keeps the catalog fresh. Product-specific landing page subdomains (champagnekit.teaspressa.com, mimosakit.teaspressa.com) show marketing sophistication. The wholesale blog coaching retailers on seasonal prep is a differentiator most CPG brands at this scale don't offer. And the premium partnerships (NetJets, Tory Burch Fellow, Greenwood Brewery) demonstrate that the brand operates at a level its digital infrastructure hasn't caught up to.

Company Profile

Teaspressa is a Phoenix-based CPG brand founded by Allison DeVane in January 2015. The company manufactures tea-based beverage products, most notably the "Luxe Mixer Cube": flavored sugar cubes that dissolve in drinks to create instant cocktails, mocktails, tea lattes, and mimosas. Allison invented a patented tea process that brews like espresso (the origin product), but the mixer cubes are the primary revenue driver.

Allison is the 100% owner with no outside investors. She started by selling tea from a laundry cart on the streets of Phoenix, pitched on Shark Tank shortly after launch, and opened the first Teaspressa retail store. The retail locations closed during COVID. The business pivoted to wholesale-first. The collabs page states she has "slowly grown the business to over seven figures with 13,000+ retailers."

Products are hand-made in Phoenix. She identifies as an inventor and scientist. Recognition includes Tory Burch Fellow and Titan 100 CEO listing.

Structured Data

JSON-LD Organization schema is present (site-wide): company name, logo, and sameAs social links. Four of eight social links in the sameAs array are empty strings, which won't cause errors but indicates incomplete configuration.

Product schema is present on product pages (tested on Vanilla Bean Tallboy): @type Product with brand, category ("Beverages"), GTIN, offers, SKU. The product schema uses "http://schema.org/" while the Organization schema uses "https://schema.org/" (mixed protocol contexts). Both are technically valid but inconsistent.

What This Is About

Teaspressa's distribution reached a scale where the infrastructure behind it matters. 13,000 retail accounts, seasonal product rotation, trade show investment, and a growing DTC channel: the operational surface area is large. The systems connecting these pieces (wholesale ordering, analytics, retention, event-based verticals) were built incrementally as the business grew. That's normal. The opportunity now is to connect them deliberately.

The work below is organized in three phases. Phase 1 addresses the wholesale infrastructure gap, which is the highest-leverage change for a business where wholesale drives the majority of revenue. Phase 2 builds the DTC retention systems that convert one-time buyers into recurring customers. Phase 3 connects the measurement layer so every decision going forward is informed by actual data.

1. Wholesale Buyer Experience
High Leverage

Build a digital line sheet and buyer portal that runs on top of the existing wholesale Shopify store. The line sheet is the tool that lives after the trade show: a retail buyer who met Allison at Atlanta Market can browse the seasonal catalog, see what's new, check pricing, and place a reorder without navigating a second e-commerce store. It looks like the brand. It works on a phone. It can be shared internally ("show this to the buyer").

  • Digital line sheet pulling product data, images, and pricing from Shopify. Seasonal collections highlighted. Shareable link per account or per collection.
  • Account-level visibility: order history, reorder shortcut, seasonal catalog preview, new product alerts.
  • Trade show follow-up sequence: after a show, every buyer scanned at the booth gets the line sheet with a personalized note and a one-click reorder path.
  • Integration with Klaviyo for wholesale-specific email flows (new product launch, seasonal transition, restock reminders).
2. DTC Retention and Recurring Revenue
Next Phase

Convert the dormant subscription infrastructure into a live product. The seasonal flavor rotation provides a natural subscription story: subscribers receive the new seasonal collection before it hits shelves. Pair this with self-service quoting for the wedding and corporate verticals, where larger order values justify the build.

  • Subscribe-and-save on key products: monthly or quarterly Mixer Cube delivery, subscriber-exclusive seasonal flavors, easy skip/swap/cancel.
  • Wedding configurator: select cube flavor, packaging, quantity, personalization. Instant quote. Reduce the quoting cycle from days to seconds.
  • Corporate gifting builder: budget-based recommendations, bulk pricing tiers, branded packaging options, recurring annual setup for holiday gifting.
  • LoyalTEA program audit: is the current points program driving repeat purchases? Are members reordering at a higher rate? If not, restructure the incentives.
3. Measurement and Analytics
Foundation

Install Google Tag Manager as the container for all tracking. Build a data layer that connects Klaviyo, Postscript, GA4, and the ad pixels into a unified event model. Clean the DNS records. Resolve the duplicate review platform situation. The goal: one coherent view of customer behavior across DTC and wholesale, so marketing decisions are informed by data rather than intuition.

  • Google Tag Manager setup with server-side tagging for accuracy and privacy compliance.
  • Unified data layer: add-to-cart, purchase, email signup, SMS opt-in, review submission, subscription activation all tracked as coordinated events.
  • Consolidate review platforms (Judge.me or Stamped.io, not both). Migrate reviews to the chosen platform.
  • TikTok audit: analyze per-video performance, identify what (if anything) works, and either restructure the content strategy or reallocate the production effort to higher-ROI channels.
  • SEO baseline: indexed page count, ranking keywords, organic traffic trend, blog content performance. Identify whether the blog is driving traffic or just filling a page.

Phase-based, transparent, built on research rather than assumptions.
