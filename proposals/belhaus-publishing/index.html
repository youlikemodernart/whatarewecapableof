A publishing house for BELHAUS.

What it would mean for the gallery to publish its own program, and how we would scope the first book: a monograph for Sherri Belassen.

The short version

Selection and amplification are yours. The rest is machinery.

A publisher does two things that cannot be handed off: it selects (decides what is worth making and putting its name on) and it amplifies (gets the work in front of more people than would otherwise see it). Everything else, printing, design, distribution, the metadata, the legal paperwork, is machinery that can be bought, built, or run for you.

BELHAUS already does the hard, un-buyable part. You select (a 25-artist roster, a monthly program, a point of view). You design (Gino is the house's designer; the catalogue covers already exist, in clay). What you have not built yet is the machinery: an imprint of record, ISBNs and trade metadata, a print relationship, a way for the books to reach people past the gallery walls. Becoming a publishing house means adding that machinery to the taste you already have. If you decide to do this, the arrangement is straightforward: you keep the taste and the design, and we run the machinery for you.

You have been circling this for years. The "catalogues that do not open," the ceramic objects with real covers and edition numbers, are the tell. You are obsessed with the catalogue as a form and have never made one that opens. The imprint is that, finally made real.

The shift

What changes when a gallery becomes a publisher

A painting is a unique object you sell once, directly, to one collector. A book is a manufactured product that moves through a supply chain. That is the shift, and it brings a few new realities:

  • A book has an identity in a system. An ISBN, a metadata record (the digital "spine label" that lets a store or library find it), a subject code, a publisher of record. This is what separates a real house's book from a self-published one. It is mostly invisible, and it is the first thing we set up for you.
  • A book reaches people through channels you do not own. Selling a few copies at the gallery is easy. Getting the book into other bookstores, art-book shops, museum shops, and onto the art-book-fair tables means either distribution (someone who warehouses and sells your list to the trade for a cut) or being orderable through the wholesale system. You do not have to do this. Plenty of gallery imprints sell direct only. But it is a real fork with real consequences, and it is one of the first questions to answer.
  • A book is made on a long clock. A monograph runs on lead times measured in months: photography, design, proofs, the print run, then the launch. Trade reviews and fair deadlines are set six months ahead. The gallery's pace is faster than the book's; the discipline is new.
  • A book is an edition, not an original. You decide the print run up front, and that decision sets the budget. Price runs about five to six times what a copy costs to make. Print too many and they sit in a closet; print too few and you reprint at a worse price. Getting the number right is its own skill.

The economics

The economics, told straight

Here is the fact every art-book publisher learns: a small art monograph almost never pays for itself on book sales alone. A run of a few thousand copies, sold at a fair price, usually does not cover what it cost to make. This is normal. It is not a sign you did it wrong.

So art books are funded by a stack, not by copy sales:

  • The gallery funds it as part of the program (the book is a marketing and legacy asset for the artist and the gallery, the way a catalogue always has been).
  • A limited edition pays for the trade edition. A signed, numbered version, sometimes with an original print or drawing, priced for collectors and pre-sold, can cover the hard costs before you print. You already sell editions and a $1,000 giclée; this is the same move.
  • The exhibition carries it. A book tied to a show shares the budget and the attention, and the show, the book, and the prints all sell each other. You run a monthly program already; this is a natural fit, the strongest card BELHAUS holds.
  • Ancillary objects (prints, merch, the ceramic pieces) ride alongside.

So the useful question is: what is this book for, and how do we fund it. For a gallery imprint the honest answers are usually: it deepens the relationship with the artist, it gives collectors something to own and the press something to write about, it documents the program, and it builds a backlist that, over years, becomes an asset in itself.

What success looks like

A coherent list, built to last

Success is a coherent list that makes your artists more visible and your gallery more serious, built to last. A bestseller is not the goal and never was. Three things to aim at:

  • The list reads as one thing. The best small publishers treat their whole list like a single book, where every title belongs and a wrong one would feel like a wrong chapter. BELHAUS already has this instinct (the program coheres, the design coheres). The imprint is your program in book form.
  • The backlist becomes the asset. One book is a project. Fifteen books, one per artist, accreted over years, is a backlist, and the backlist is where the long-run value sits. Night Gallery, your model, has about seventeen titles built up exactly this way: a softcover monograph per artist, around sixty dollars, sold off their own site, each one extending an exhibition. That is an achievable, repeatable shape, and your 25-artist roster is the pipeline.
  • The books get the work noticed. A monograph puts an artist's work in a form that travels: into hands, onto shelves, into the hands of writers and curators who would never see the show. For a regional gallery, that reach is the point.

The division of labor

What BELHAUS keeps, and what we run

You keep everything that is you:

  • Selection: which artists, which work, which books.
  • Design: Gino and Chloe; the books look like BELHAUS.
  • The artist relationships and the taste calls.

We run the machinery you would otherwise have to learn or hire:

  • The imprint-of-record setup, ISBNs, and trade metadata.
  • Production management and print-buying (specs, getting and comparing printer quotes, scheduling, the press check).
  • Distribution access, if and when you want reach past the gallery.
  • The title budgets and P&Ls, the contracts and rights paperwork (drafted for your approval), and the launch plan.
  • The systems and habits that let one small team run a real imprint without it becoming a second full-time job.

Nothing ships, no money moves, no file goes to a printer, without your sign-off. We draft and plan; you decide.

Where this starts

One book, done properly

The Sherri monograph is the right first title: the gallery's own founding painter, a 35-year body of work, a real story (the balance and the horizon line she found flying as a kid). It will teach the whole process on a project you care about, and it sets the template for every book after it. The questions in the next tab are how we scope it.

Scope the first book

Discovery

The Sherri Belassen book

These are the questions that scope the first book. They are grouped, with the few that gate everything else flagged first, and each carries a one-line reason for why it matters. Nothing here commits BELHAUS to anything; this is scoping.

What the research already answered, so we do not have to ask: Sherri is a Phoenix painter, a 35-year career, more than 100 works, six galleries of representation, a coherent body (desert, sky, surf, feet) organized around balance and the horizon line she found flying in her dad's plane as a kid. There is a monograph in it, comfortably. The two real constraints are pictures and words, not the amount of art.

Answer these three first

They set the budget and the schedule

01

How big is the book?

A focused recent-work monograph (60 to 80 paintings, 96 to 128 pages) or a career survey (100 to 150 paintings, 160 to 200-plus pages)?

Why: this single choice sets the page count, the print cost, the price, the amount of photography, and how much writing is needed. Everything downstream keys off it.

02

Do good photographs of the paintings already exist?

Specifically: are there professional, color-accurate, high-resolution files of the work, with Sherri or any of her six galleries? The images on her site are phone and gallery documentation, fine for the web, not big enough for full-page printing.

Why: this is the biggest cost-and-risk question in the whole project. If good files exist, we are mostly set. If not, the paintings need to be professionally re-photographed, and many are sold and spread across galleries and collectors, so we have to find and reach them. Whoever re-photographs should sign the images over to the imprint so the book can use them.

03

What is the book for, how is it funded, and who can it reach?

Is it a gallery-funded book to honor and promote Sherri and her collectors; a collector edition we pre-sell to cover costs; or a book we want in bookstores and art-book shops past the gallery? It can be more than one, but the lead intent sets the model. And roughly how many buyers can you reach directly, through the gallery, the collector and mailing lists, and Sherri's six galleries? Does the gallery fund the book, or does Sherri share the cost?

Why: this decides the print run, whether we need ISBNs and distribution, and where the money comes from. The reachable-buyer number is the one that sizes the run and tells us whether a pre-sold collector edition can cover the hard costs. An art monograph rarely earns back its cost on sales, so naming the purpose, the audience, and the funding up front is how we size it sanely.

The book itself

Spine, selection, title

04

What is the one-sentence version of this book?

If we had to say what it is in a line, what is it? And is the organizing idea the "balance and the horizon" story (her recurring exhibition title was Balance), or a straight survey of the work?

Why: the spine of the book. The balance and horizon origin story is a strong, ready-made organizing idea and a candidate title.

05

Is there a working title?

06

Who picks the paintings, and roughly how many?

Sherri, Gino, the two of you, or an outside editor?

Why: plate selection is the core editorial act of an art book; worth naming who holds the pen.

The words

Text and catalog data

07

Who writes the essay?

Right now there is almost no text written, just a short bio and a CV. A monograph usually wants an introduction or an essay. Options: an outside critic or curator (gives outside credibility), Gino, Sherri in her own voice, or an interview. Is there a writer in mind, and a budget for a commissioned essay?

Why: the text is commissioned from scratch, so it needs a decision and a lead time. An outside essayist also widens the audience.

08

Does Sherri have the catalog data for captions, and the right to reproduce the work?

Each plate needs a title, year, medium, dimensions, and current owner or credit. The site gives titles and sizes but not dates or media. And for works now owned by collectors or museums, does Sherri keep the right to reproduce them in a book, or do we need the owner's permission?

Why: caption data is slow to assemble after the fact and easy to gather now; missing dates and credits stall a book at the proofs stage, and reproduction permission for sold works is the kind of thing that surfaces late and holds up a printer if we do not settle it early.

The object

Form and edition

09

What should the book feel like in the hand?

Softcover (like the $60 Night Gallery monographs) or a hardcover; what trim and heft? And do we want to use her panoramic paintings (some are three times as wide as they are tall) as gatefolds or full spreads?

Why: the object decision is also a budget and a design decision; her format range is unusually wide and is a real opportunity for the design.

10

Do we want a limited edition alongside the regular one?

A signed, numbered version, possibly with a small original work or a print, priced for collectors and sold first.

Why: this is the cleanest way to pre-fund the hard costs, and Sherri already has a $1,000 giclee in her shop, so the collector appetite is proven.

The frame and the timeline

Name, date, design

11

Under what name does it publish?

BELHAUS directly, or a named imprint (BELHAUS Editions, or something with house meaning)?

Why: this is the imprint's first identity decision.

Our recommendation on the trade-vs-direct call, so you are reacting to a plan rather than starting cold: sell the first book gallery-direct, but give it its own ISBN and clean metadata from the start, so later books can reach bookstores and art-book shops without redoing the foundation. Owning your own ISBN from book one costs little and forecloses nothing, so your answer to question 3 sets how hard we lean toward the trade; the main open piece here is just whether the imprint carries the BELHAUS name or its own.

12

Is there a target date or a show to tie it to?

The natural launch is a BELHAUS Sherri exhibition: the show, the book, and the prints all sell each other, and it sets a real deadline.

Why: a book tied to a show shares the budget and the attention, and a date turns the plan into a schedule.

13

Confirm Gino and Chloe design the book in-house.

Why: assumed, and it is a real advantage (BELHAUS owns the design), but worth confirming so we plan production around their design rather than supplying it.

What we do with the answers

The first three answers let us draft a budget and schedule for the book. The rest let us write the spec, line up photography, and brief the essay.
